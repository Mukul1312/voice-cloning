How to read this — the 3 axes of a voice (click to collapse)

One voice, shown three ways. Each panel isolates one thing your ear normally blends together — and the readout puts a number on it. A voice = cadence (its tune) + timbre (its colour) + accent (how it shapes sounds). They're independent: two people can share an accent and a cadence and still sound different — that leftover is timbre.

① CADENCE — his melody & pacing  top · Pitch panel · green line
What: how high or low the voice is, instant to instant. That's pitch = F0 (fundamental frequency), in Hz — his vocal folds open/close ~130 times a second, so ~130 Hz. Watch: the green line rises on stressed / emphatic words and falls at the end of a phrase. That rise-and-fall shape is exactly the "pace" you already hear — you're just seeing it now.

② TIMBRE — his tone-colour  bottom-left · Spectrum slice
What: what makes him sound like him, separate from pitch or words. His vocal-tract shape (throat, mouth) resonates — boosting certain frequency bands called formants. The set of those boosts is his timbre. Watch: the curve's overall shape; the bumps are the formants (F1/F2/F3 are marked). As he moves between sounds the bumps slide — that sliding shape is timbre in motion. (The spectrogram just above is the same information drawn as a heat-map over time.)

③ ACCENT — how he forms vowels  bottom-right · Vowel map
What: every vowel is (roughly) a single point fixed by its first two formants — F1 ≈ how open the mouth is, F2 ≈ how far forward the tongue is. ● = his current vowel; ○ = where a standard English speaker puts each vowel (heed, hid, head, had…). Watch: when his white dot lands away from the grey reference points, that gap is his accent — e.g. an "ah" sitting somewhere English doesn't put it.

The readout (the line just above the panels) shows live t · F0 · F1 · F2 · F3 at the cursor, so every sound has a number attached.
